01Descriptive Statistics

Summarizing data. Central tendency tells you where; dispersion tells you how spread; shape tells you the silhouette.

Central Tendency
Arithmetic Mean
μ = (1/n) Σxᵢ
Sum all, divide by count. Sensitive to outliers.
Weighted Mean
x̄_w = Σwᵢxᵢ / Σwᵢ
Each observation contributes proportionally to its weight.
Geometric Mean
GM = (∏xᵢ)^(1/n)
For rates, ratios, growth. Always ≤ arithmetic mean.
Harmonic Mean
HM = n / Σ(1/xᵢ)
For averaging rates (speed, P/E ratios). F1 score uses this.
Median
Middle value when sorted
Robust to outliers. If n even: average of two middle values.
Mode
Most frequent value
Only measure of central tendency for categorical data. Can have 0, 1, or many.
Trimmed Mean
Mean after removing top/bottom k%
Compromise between mean (efficient) and median (robust).
Dispersion
Range
Range = max − min
Simplest measure. Completely determined by two extreme values.
Variance (population)
σ² = (1/n) Σ(xᵢ − μ)²
Average squared deviation. Units are squared.
Variance (sample)
s² = (1/(n−1)) Σ(xᵢ − x̄)²
Bessel's correction (n−1) removes bias from using x̄ instead of μ.
Standard Deviation
σ = √σ²   or   s = √s²
Same units as data. ~68% within ±1σ for normal distributions.
IQR
IQR = Q3 − Q1
Middle 50% of data. Outliers: below Q1−1.5·IQR or above Q3+1.5·IQR.
MAD (Median Absolute Deviation)
MAD = median(|xᵢ − median(x)|)
Most robust dispersion measure. Unaffected by any single outlier.
Coefficient of Variation
CV = σ / μ
Relative variability. Dimensionless — compare spread across different scales.
Standard Error
SE = s / √n
Std dev of the sampling distribution of the mean. Shrinks with more data.
Shape
Skewness
γ₁ = (1/n) Σ((xᵢ − μ)/σ)³
0 = symmetric. Positive = right tail longer. Negative = left tail longer.
Kurtosis
γ₂ = (1/n) Σ((xᵢ − μ)/σ)⁴
Normal = 3 (excess kurtosis = 0). Higher = heavier tails, more extreme outliers.
Relationship
Covariance
Cov(X,Y) = (1/(n−1)) Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)
Direction of linear relationship. Scale-dependent — hard to compare across datasets.
Pearson Correlation
r = Cov(X,Y) / (sₓ · sᵧ)
Normalized covariance. [−1, +1]. Measures linear association only.
Spearman Rank Correlation
ρₛ = Pearson(rank(X), rank(Y))
Measures monotonic (not just linear) relationship. Robust to outliers.
Kendall's Tau
τ = (concordant − discordant) / C(n,2)
Proportion of concordant pairs. More robust than Spearman for small n.
Partial Correlation
r_XY·Z = (r_XY − r_XZ·r_YZ) / √((1−r²_XZ)(1−r²_YZ))
Correlation between X and Y after removing the effect of Z.
Point-Biserial Correlation
Same formula as Pearson, one variable binary
Correlation between a continuous and a dichotomous variable.
02Probability Foundations

The language of uncertainty. Every ML model is, at its core, a probability machine.

Probability
P(A) = |A| / |Ω|
For equally likely outcomes. Always 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1.
Kolmogorov Axioms
1) P(A) ≥ 0
2) P(Ω) = 1
3) P(A∪B) = P(A)+P(B) if disjoint
The three axioms from which all probability theory follows.
Complement
P(A') = 1 − P(A)
Addition Rule
P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B)
Inclusion-exclusion. If mutually exclusive: P(A∪B) = P(A)+P(B).
Multiplication Rule
P(A∩B) = P(A) · P(B|A)
If independent: P(A∩B) = P(A) · P(B).
Conditional Probability
P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B)
Probability of A given B has occurred. The basis of Bayesian reasoning.
Bayes' Theorem
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)
Update beliefs with evidence. posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
Law of Total Probability
P(B) = Σᵢ P(B|Aᵢ) · P(Aᵢ)
Compute P(B) by summing over all partitions Aᵢ of sample space.
Independence
P(A∩B) = P(A) · P(B)
Knowing A gives no information about B. P(A|B) = P(A).
Conditional Independence
P(A∩B|C) = P(A|C) · P(B|C)
A and B independent once C is known. Key assumption of Naive Bayes.
Expected Value
E[X] = Σ xᵢ P(xᵢ)   (discrete)
E[X] = ∫ x f(x) dx   (continuous)
Long-run average. Linear: E[aX+b] = aE[X]+b.
Variance via Expectation
Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²
Computational shortcut. Var(aX+b) = a²Var(X).
Covariance via Expectation
Cov(X,Y) = E[XY] − E[X]E[Y]
If independent: Cov(X,Y) = 0. Converse is NOT true.
Moment Generating Function
M_X(t) = E[e^(tX)]
k-th moment = M^(k)(0). Uniquely determines the distribution.
03Combinatorics

Counting outcomes — the machinery behind discrete probability.

Factorial
n! = n × (n−1) × … × 1
0! = 1 by convention. Grows explosively: 10! = 3,628,800.
Permutations (ordered)
P(n,k) = n! / (n−k)!
Choose k items from n, order matters.
Combinations (unordered)
C(n,k) = n! / (k!(n−k)!)
"n choose k." Order doesn't matter. C(n,k) = C(n, n−k).
Multinomial Coefficient
n! / (n₁! n₂! … nₖ!)
Ways to divide n items into groups of sizes n₁, n₂, …, nₖ.
04Probability Distributions

The building blocks. Every model assumption, every test statistic, every generative process maps to one of these.

Discrete Distributions
Bernoulli
P(X=1) = p, P(X=0) = 1−p
Single trial, two outcomes. E = p, Var = p(1−p).
Binomial
P(X=k) = C(n,k) p^k (1−p)^(n−k)
k successes in n independent trials. E = np, Var = np(1−p).
Poisson
P(X=k) = λ^k e^(−λ) / k!
Counts in a fixed interval. E = Var = λ. Limit of Binomial as n→∞, p→0, np→λ.
Geometric
P(X=k) = (1−p)^(k−1) p
Trials until first success. E = 1/p, Var = (1−p)/p². Memoryless.
Negative Binomial
P(X=k) = C(k−1, r−1) p^r (1−p)^(k−r)
Trials until r-th success. E = r/p. Generalizes geometric (r=1).
Hypergeometric
P(X=k) = C(K,k)C(N−K,n−k) / C(N,n)
Sampling without replacement. N total, K successes, draw n. E = nK/N.
Multinomial
P(x₁,…,xₖ) = (n! / ∏xᵢ!) ∏pᵢ^xᵢ
k categories, n trials. Generalizes binomial. Used in NLP (bag of words).
Discrete Uniform
P(X=k) = 1/n for k=1,…,n
All outcomes equally likely. Fair die: n=6. E = (n+1)/2.
Continuous Distributions
Uniform
f(x) = 1/(b−a) for a ≤ x ≤ b
Constant density. E = (a+b)/2, Var = (b−a)²/12.
Normal (Gaussian)
f(x) = (1/(σ√(2π))) exp(−(x−μ)²/(2σ²))
Bell curve. 68-95-99.7 rule. Sum of normals is normal. CLT target.
Standard Normal
Z = (X−μ)/σ ~ N(0,1)
Any normal → standard normal via z-score. Tables, p-values live here.
Log-Normal
If ln(X) ~ N(μ,σ²), then X ~ LogN
Always positive, right-skewed. Models: stock prices, income, city sizes.
Exponential
f(x) = λe^(−λx) for x ≥ 0
Time between Poisson events. E = 1/λ, Var = 1/λ². Memoryless.
Gamma
f(x) = (β^α / Γ(α)) x^(α−1) e^(−βx)
Sum of α independent exponentials. Generalizes exponential (α=1) and chi-square.
Beta
f(x) = x^(α−1)(1−x)^(β−1) / B(α,β)
Defined on [0,1]. Perfect for modeling probabilities. Conjugate prior for Bernoulli/Binomial.
Chi-Square (χ²)
χ² ~ Gamma(k/2, 1/2)
Sum of k squared standard normals. df = k. Used in goodness-of-fit, independence tests.
Student's t
t = Z / √(V/ν), V~χ²(ν)
Heavier tails than normal. Approaches normal as ν→∞. Used when σ unknown.
F-distribution
F = (V₁/d₁) / (V₂/d₂)
Ratio of two chi-squares. Used in ANOVA and regression F-tests.
Weibull
f(x) = (k/λ)(x/λ)^(k−1) e^(−(x/λ)^k)
Flexible failure-time model. k=1 → exponential. Models lifetime, wind speed.
Pareto
f(x) = αx_m^α / x^(α+1) for x ≥ x_m
Power law / heavy tail. 80-20 rule. Models wealth, city populations, file sizes.
05Central Limit Theorem & Sampling
Central Limit Theorem
x̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) as n → ∞
Sample means approach normal regardless of population shape. Practically kicks in around n ≥ 30.
Sampling Distribution of x̄
E[x̄] = μ,   Var(x̄) = σ²/n
The mean of means equals the population mean. Variance shrinks with sample size.
Law of Large Numbers
x̄_n → μ as n → ∞
Sample mean converges to population mean. Foundation of all empirical estimation.
Bootstrap
Resample with replacement B times, compute statistic each time
Distribution-free inference. Get CI and SE for any statistic without assumptions.
06Estimation Theory

How to go from sample to population. Point estimates, intervals, and the properties that make an estimator good.

Maximum Likelihood Estimation
θ̂_MLE = argmax_θ ∏ f(xᵢ|θ)
= argmax_θ Σ log f(xᵢ|θ)
Find θ that makes the observed data most probable. Take the log, differentiate, set to zero.
Method of Moments
Set sample moments = population moments, solve for θ
Simpler than MLE, often less efficient. Match x̄ to E[X], s² to Var(X), etc.
MAP Estimation
θ̂_MAP = argmax_θ P(θ|data)
= argmax_θ [log L(data|θ) + log P(θ)]
MLE + prior. With uniform prior, MAP = MLE. With Gaussian prior, MAP = Ridge regression.
Confidence Interval
x̄ ± z* · (s/√n)
95% CI: z*=1.96. 99%: z*=2.576. Interpretation: if you repeat the experiment ∞ times, 95% of CIs contain μ.
Properties of Estimators
Unbiased
E[θ̂] = θ
On average, the estimator hits the true value.
Consistent
θ̂ → θ as n → ∞
Converges to truth with more data.
Efficient
Var(θ̂) = 1/I(θ) (Cramér-Rao bound)
Achieves minimum possible variance among all unbiased estimators.
MSE of an Estimator
MSE(θ̂) = Bias(θ̂)² + Var(θ̂)
Total error = systematic error + random error. Sometimes biased estimators have lower MSE.
07Hypothesis Testing

The formal framework for deciding whether a pattern is real or just noise.

Framework
H₀: null hypothesis (no effect)
H₁: alternative (effect exists)
Choose α → compute test stat → get p-value → decide
p-value
P(data ≥ observed | H₀ true)
NOT the probability H₀ is true. It's how surprising the data is if H₀ were true.
Z-test
z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ/√n)
σ known or n≥30. Tests if population mean = μ₀.
One-sample t-test
t = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s/√n), df = n−1
σ unknown. Same purpose as z-test but with heavier tails.
Two-sample t-test
t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
Compare means of two independent groups. Welch's version doesn't assume equal variance.
Paired t-test
t = d̄ / (s_d/√n), d = x₁−x₂
Same subjects measured twice (before/after). Test if mean difference ≠ 0.
Chi-square Goodness of Fit
χ² = Σ (Oᵢ − Eᵢ)² / Eᵢ
Does observed frequency match expected distribution? df = k−1.
Chi-square Independence
Same formula, applied to contingency table
Are two categorical variables associated? df = (r−1)(c−1).
Error Types Type I (α): reject H₀ when it's true (false positive). You see a pattern that isn't there.
Type II (β): fail to reject H₀ when it's false (false negative). You miss a real pattern.
Power = 1 − β: probability of detecting a real effect. Increase with larger n, larger effect, or larger α.
Multiple Testing
Bonferroni Correction
α_adjusted = α / m
m = number of tests. Conservative — controls family-wise error rate.
Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR)
Rank p-values, reject if pᵢ ≤ (i/m)·α
Controls false discovery rate instead of family-wise rate. Less conservative, more power.
Effect Size (Cohen's d)
d = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / s_pooled
Standardized difference. Small=0.2, Medium=0.5, Large=0.8. p-value says if; effect size says how much.
08ANOVA

Comparing means across 3+ groups — because running pairwise t-tests inflates your Type I error.

One-Way ANOVA
F = MS_between / MS_within
MS = SS / df
H₀: all group means equal. Large F → at least one group differs.
SS_between
SS_B = Σ nⱼ(x̄ⱼ − x̄)²
Variation explained by group membership. df = k−1.
SS_within
SS_W = ΣΣ (xᵢⱼ − x̄ⱼ)²
Variation within groups (noise). df = N−k.
Two-Way ANOVA
Tests main effects of A, B, and interaction A×B
Can detect if the effect of one factor depends on the level of another.
09Nonparametric Methods

When your data doesn't meet parametric assumptions — no normality required.

Mann-Whitney U
U = Σ ranks of group 1 − n₁(n₁+1)/2
Nonparametric alternative to two-sample t-test. Compares distributions, not just means.
Wilcoxon Signed-Rank
Rank |differences|, sum positive & negative ranks
Alternative to paired t-test. Tests if median difference = 0.
Kruskal-Wallis
H = (12/(N(N+1))) Σ (Rⱼ²/nⱼ) − 3(N+1)
Alternative to one-way ANOVA. Tests if k groups come from same distribution.
Permutation Test
Shuffle labels B times, compute test stat each time
Exact test under H₀. No distributional assumptions. p = fraction of permutations ≥ observed.
Kolmogorov-Smirnov
D = max|F_n(x) − F(x)|
Tests if sample comes from a specific distribution. Compares empirical vs theoretical CDF.
Kernel Density Estimation
f̂(x) = (1/(nh)) Σ K((x−xᵢ)/h)
Smooth nonparametric density estimate. K = kernel (usually Gaussian), h = bandwidth.
10Bayesian Statistics

Parameters aren't fixed unknown constants — they're random variables with distributions that update as data arrives.

Bayesian Inference
P(θ|data) = P(data|θ) · P(θ) / P(data)
posterior = likelihood × prior / evidence. Everything follows from this.
Prior → Posterior
posterior ∝ likelihood × prior
P(data) is just a normalizing constant. Often we work with the unnormalized form.
Conjugate Priors
Beta → Bernoulli/Binomial
Normal → Normal (known σ)
Gamma → Poisson
Dirichlet → Multinomial
Prior and posterior are in the same family → closed-form updates. No MCMC needed.
Beta-Binomial Update
Prior: Beta(α, β)
After k successes in n trials:
Posterior: Beta(α+k, β+n−k)
The most important conjugate pair. Models uncertainty about probabilities (A/B testing, CTR).
Credible Interval
Find [a,b] s.t. P(a ≤ θ ≤ b | data) = 0.95
Direct probability statement about θ. "There's a 95% probability θ is in this interval." (CI can't say this.)
Bayesian vs Frequentist
Bayesian: P(θ|data) — probability of parameter
Frequentist: P(data|θ) — probability of data
Different questions, not competitors. Use whichever matches your problem.
11Information Theory

Quantifying uncertainty, surprise, and the information content of data. Powers decision trees, cross-entropy loss, and generative models.

Entropy
H(X) = −Σ P(xᵢ) log₂ P(xᵢ)
Average surprise. Max when all outcomes equally likely (uniform). 0 when outcome is certain.
Joint Entropy
H(X,Y) = −ΣΣ P(x,y) log P(x,y)
Uncertainty of the joint system. H(X,Y) ≤ H(X) + H(Y), equality iff independent.
Conditional Entropy
H(X|Y) = H(X,Y) − H(Y)
Remaining uncertainty about X after observing Y.
Mutual Information
I(X;Y) = H(X) − H(X|Y) = H(X) + H(Y) − H(X,Y)
Information gained about X by observing Y. Symmetric: I(X;Y) = I(Y;X). 0 iff independent.
KL Divergence
D_KL(P‖Q) = Σ P(x) log(P(x)/Q(x))
Information lost when Q approximates P. Not symmetric. Always ≥ 0. Used in VAEs, policy gradient.
Cross-Entropy
H(P,Q) = −Σ P(x) log Q(x)
H(P,Q) = H(P) + D_KL(P‖Q). Minimizing cross-entropy = minimizing KL (since H(P) is fixed).
Information Gain
IG(Y,X) = H(Y) − H(Y|X)
Reduction in entropy from splitting on feature X. Used to build decision trees (ID3/C4.5).
Gini Impurity
Gini = 1 − Σ pᵢ²
Alternative to entropy for tree splits. Faster to compute (no log). Max = 0.5 (binary), 0 = pure.
12Regression
Simple Linear Regression
ŷ = β₀ + β₁x
Multiple Linear Regression
ŷ = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βₚxₚ
OLS Slope
β₁ = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²
= Cov(X,Y) / Var(X). Closed-form solution.
OLS Intercept
β₀ = ȳ − β₁x̄
Line always passes through (x̄, ȳ).
Normal Equation
β = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy
Closed-form for multiple regression. O(p³) — expensive for many features.
Cost Function (MSE)
J(β) = (1/2m) Σ(ŷᵢ − yᵢ)²
The ½ makes the derivative cleaner. Gradient descent minimizes this.
Gradient Descent Update
βⱼ := βⱼ − α · (1/m) Σ(ŷᵢ−yᵢ)·xⱼᵢ
α = learning rate. Repeat until convergence. Update all β simultaneously.
Polynomial Regression
ŷ = β₀ + β₁x + β₂x² + … + βₖx^k
Still linear in the parameters — just use x, x², x³ as separate features.
13Classification
Sigmoid (Logistic)
σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(−z))
Maps ℝ → (0,1). Output = probability of class 1.
Logistic Regression
P(y=1|x) = σ(βᵀx)
Linear in log-odds: log(p/(1−p)) = βᵀx. Decision boundary at βᵀx = 0.
Binary Cross-Entropy (Log Loss)
J = −(1/m) Σ[yᵢ log(ŷᵢ) + (1−yᵢ) log(1−ŷᵢ)]
Convex. Heavily penalizes confident wrong predictions.
Softmax (multi-class)
P(y=k|x) = e^(zₖ) / Σⱼ e^(zⱼ)
Generalizes sigmoid to K classes. Outputs sum to 1.
Naive Bayes
P(y|x₁,…,xₚ) ∝ P(y) ∏ P(xⱼ|y)
Assumes features independent given class. Fast, works well for text. "Naive" = the independence assumption.
KNN
Predict class of k nearest neighbors (majority vote)
No training phase. k=1 overfits, large k underfits. Distance metric matters (usually Euclidean).
14Regression Metrics
MAE
MAE = (1/n) Σ|yᵢ − ŷᵢ|
Same units as y. Robust to outliers. Not differentiable at 0.
MSE
MSE = (1/n) Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
Units = y². Punishes large errors. Differentiable everywhere.
RMSE
RMSE = √MSE
Same units as y. Most reported metric. RMSE ≥ MAE always.
MAPE
MAPE = (100/n) Σ |yᵢ−ŷᵢ|/|yᵢ|
Percentage error. Undefined when yᵢ=0. Scale-independent.
R²
R² = 1 − SS_res / SS_total
% variance explained. 1=perfect, 0=same as mean, <0=worse than mean.
Adjusted R²
Adj R² = 1 − [(1−R²)(n−1)/(n−p−1)]
Penalizes adding useless features. If Adj R² drops, remove the feature.
15Classification Metrics
Accuracy
(TP+TN) / (TP+TN+FP+FN)
Misleading on imbalanced data.
Precision
TP / (TP+FP)
"Of predicted positives, how many are correct?" Minimize false alarms.
Recall (Sensitivity)
TP / (TP+FN)
"Of actual positives, how many did I catch?" Minimize misses.
F1 Score
F1 = 2·(P·R)/(P+R)
Harmonic mean of precision and recall. 0 to 1.
Fβ Score
Fβ = (1+β²)·(P·R)/(β²P+R)
β>1 weights recall more, β<1 weights precision more. F1 is β=1.
Specificity (TNR)
TN / (TN+FP)
"Of actual negatives, how many correctly identified?"
AUC-ROC
Area under TPR vs FPR curve
0.5=random, 1.0=perfect. Threshold-independent. Probability that a random positive ranks above a random negative.
AUC-PR
Area under Precision vs Recall curve
Better than AUC-ROC for highly imbalanced datasets. Focuses on the positive class.
Log Loss
−(1/n) Σ[yᵢ log(pᵢ) + (1−yᵢ)log(1−pᵢ)]
Penalizes confident wrong predictions exponentially. Lower = better.
Cohen's Kappa
κ = (p₀ − pₑ) / (1 − pₑ)
Agreement beyond chance. p₀=observed accuracy, pₑ=expected by chance. 0=chance, 1=perfect.
16Regularization

Trading a little bias for a lot of variance reduction. The antidote to overfitting.

L1 — Lasso
J = MSE + λ Σ|βⱼ|
Drives coefficients to exactly 0 → feature selection. Creates sparse models.
L2 — Ridge
J = MSE + λ Σβⱼ²
Shrinks toward 0 but never reaches it. Handles multicollinearity. Equivalent to Gaussian prior (MAP).
Elastic Net
J = MSE + λ₁Σ|βⱼ| + λ₂Σβⱼ²
Best of both: sparsity (L1) + stability (L2). Two hyperparameters.
Dropout
Randomly zero out neurons with prob p during training
Neural network regularization. Forces redundancy. Like training an ensemble. p typically 0.2–0.5.
Early Stopping
Stop training when validation loss starts increasing
Implicit regularization. Monitor val loss, keep best weights.
λ (regularization strength) λ=0 → no regularization (plain OLS). λ→∞ → all coefficients→0 (underfitting). Find the sweet spot with cross-validation.
17PCA & Dimensionality Reduction
PCA Goal
Find directions of maximum variance in the data
Project onto top k principal components. Reduce dimensions while preserving information.
Steps
1) Center data (subtract mean)
2) Compute covariance matrix C = XᵀX/(n−1)
3) Eigendecompose: Cv = λv
4) Sort by λ, keep top k eigenvectors
5) Project: Z = XW_k
Variance Explained
VE_k = λₖ / Σλᵢ
Fraction of total variance captured by k-th component. Cumulative sum → choose k.
SVD
X = UΣVᵀ
U = left singular vectors, Σ = singular values, V = right singular vectors. PCA uses V and Σ.
Eigenvalue / Eigenvector
Av = λv
v = direction unchanged by A, λ = how much A stretches v. Core of PCA, spectral methods, PageRank.
Scree Plot
Plot eigenvalues in decreasing order
Look for the "elbow" — where additional components stop adding meaningful variance.
Key Linear Algebra
Dot Product
a·b = Σaᵢbᵢ = |a||b|cos(θ)
Scalar. Foundation of projections and similarity.
L1 Norm (Manhattan)
‖x‖₁ = Σ|xᵢ|
Used in Lasso regularization.
L2 Norm (Euclidean)
‖x‖₂ = √(Σxᵢ²)
Used in Ridge, KNN, cosine similarity.
Cosine Similarity
cos(θ) = (a·b) / (‖a‖‖b‖)
Direction similarity, ignores magnitude. [−1,1]. Standard in NLP (word embeddings, TF-IDF).
18Key Inequalities & Theorems

The theoretical guarantees that underpin everything above.

Markov's Inequality
P(X ≥ a) ≤ E[X] / a   (X ≥ 0)
Weakest bound, fewest assumptions. Only needs X ≥ 0 and E[X] exists.
Chebyshev's Inequality
P(|X−μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k²
At most 1/k² of data lies more than k std devs from the mean. Any distribution.
Jensen's Inequality
f(E[X]) ≤ E[f(X)] if f is convex
Convex function of expectation ≤ expectation of convex function. Used in variational inference (ELBO).
Cramér-Rao Lower Bound
Var(θ̂) ≥ 1 / I(θ)
I(θ) = Fisher information. No unbiased estimator can have variance below this bound.
Bias-Variance Decomposition
E[(y−ŷ)²] = Bias²+Var+σ²
Total error = (systematic error)² + estimation uncertainty + irreducible noise.
Central Limit Theorem
√n(x̄−μ)/σ → N(0,1)
Standardized sample mean converges to standard normal. The reason statistics works.
Law of Large Numbers
x̄_n →ᵖ μ
Weak LLN: convergence in probability. Strong LLN: almost sure convergence.
Gauss-Markov Theorem
OLS is BLUE under classical assumptions
Best Linear Unbiased Estimator — minimum variance among all linear unbiased estimators. Requires: linearity, exogeneity, homoscedasticity, no multicollinearity.
